1. Why Barranquilla — and Why It Matters for Your Shipment

Barranquilla sits at the mouth of the Magdalena River on Colombia’s Caribbean coast and is one of the country’s four major maritime ports, alongside Cartagena, Santa Marta, and Buenaventura. For anyone relocating to the Atlántico region — or to nearby cities like Santa Marta, Sincelejo, or Valledupar — shipping directly to Barranquilla usually beats routing through Cartagena. You avoid a second inland customs handoff plus the trucking fees that come with it.

A few logistics realities shape every decision you’ll make:

  • Puerto de Barranquilla is a river port, not a deep-water port. Most household-goods carriers call there directly, but some specialty lines prefer Cartagena. If your freight forwarder routes through Cartagena, you’ll pay more for inland trucking and a second round of port fees.
  • Ernesto Cortissoz International Airport (BAQ) handles air cargo. For pets and urgent personal effects, this is usually your entry point.
  • DIAN (Dirección de Impuestos y Aduanas Nacionales) is Colombia’s customs authority. Every shipment clears through DIAN, and the rules are the same nationwide — but local broker quality and port efficiency vary significantly.
  • ICA (Instituto Colombiano Agropecuario) handles animal and plant imports, including pets.
  • Invima regulates food, medicine, and cosmetics imports.

Rule of thumb: If you live west of Barranquilla (Cartagena, Montería), Cartagena may be cheaper. If you’re going to Barranquilla, Santa Marta, or anywhere east along the coast, shipping directly to Barranquilla saves time and money.

2. Shipping Methods Compared

Your choice among these methods depends on volume, budget, and urgency. Most relocations use a combination — for example, sea freight for the bulk of household goods, air freight for anything you’ll need in the first month, and a courier for documents and small valuables.

Method Best for Transit Typical cost Notes
Sea freight — FCL (Full Container Load) Whole-home moves, 3+ bedroom households 18–35 days door-to-port $4,500–$9,000 (20′ container) You pay for exclusive use of a 20′ or 40′ container. Most cost-efficient per cubic meter.
Sea freight — LCL (Less than Container Load) Partial moves, studios, 1–2 bedrooms 25–45 days (shares consolidation) $1,500–$4,500 You share a container with other shippers. Slower because of consolidation and deconsolidation.
Air freight Urgent items, high-value pieces, 1–10 boxes 3–7 days $6–$15 per kg Priced by chargeable weight (whichever is greater of actual or dimensional weight).
Courier (DHL, FedEx, UPS) Documents, electronics, small personal items 2–5 business days $80–$400 per box Door-to-door including customs brokerage. Best for items under ~30 kg.
Postal (USPS / Canada Post) Paperwork, very small parcels 1–4 weeks (unpredictable) $30–$150 Cheapest, but tracking and delivery reliability to Colombia are weak. Not recommended for anything valuable.
Ro-Ro (Roll-on/Roll-off) Vehicles only (where permitted) 15–30 days $1,200–$2,800 Vehicle is driven onto a specialized ship. See vehicles section — Colombia heavily restricts imports.

Sea Freight in Depth

A 20-foot container holds roughly 28–30 cubic meters of packed household goods — enough for a 2-bedroom apartment. A 40-foot container holds about 58–60 cubic meters and suits a 3- or 4-bedroom house with garage items. If you’re between sizes, LCL with a reputable consolidator is usually cheaper than half-filling a 20-foot box.

There are two inland-cost models you’ll see quoted:

  • Port-to-port: The carrier gets your container to Puerto de Barranquilla. You are responsible for customs clearance, port handling charges (THC), and onward trucking. Cheaper headline price, more work and risk.
  • Door-to-door: The mover handles pickup in North America, export, ocean transit, Colombian customs clearance, and delivery to your new address. Always get an itemized breakdown — some “door-to-door” quotes exclude destination charges that can add $1,000+ at the back end.

Air Freight in Depth

Air freight is priced on chargeable weight, which is the higher of actual weight in kilograms or dimensional weight (length × width × height in cm ÷ 6,000). This means a 30 kg box of pillows costs far more than 30 kg of books. Consolidate dense items for air; save bulky light items for sea.

Ernesto Cortissoz (BAQ) handles general air cargo, but the volume is smaller than Bogotá (BOG) or Medellín (MDE). Some carriers route cargo through Bogotá and truck to Barranquilla — confirm this in your quote because it adds 2–4 days and inland handling fees.

Courier vs. Freight: Where the Line Is

Courier services are technically air freight, but they bundle pickup, export, customs brokerage, duties prepayment, and last-mile delivery into a single door-to-door rate. For shipments under roughly 30 kg and under USD $2,000 in declared value, courier is almost always simpler and only marginally more expensive than traditional air freight. Above that, traditional freight with a dedicated customs broker starts to make sense.

3. Household Goods and the Menaje de Casa Exemption

This is the single most important concept in the entire guide, and the one most new arrivals misunderstand. Colombian law allows qualifying residents to import their used household goods and personal effects under a special customs regime called menaje de casa, which can substantially reduce or eliminate import duties on those goods.

Who Qualifies

The exemption is generally available to:

  • Colombians returning to live in Colombia after at least two continuous years abroad
  • Foreign nationals moving to Colombia with a valid resident visa (Migrante or Residente category, e.g. M-visa or R-visa)
  • Diplomats and certain categories of workers under specific visas (separate regime)

Tourist-visa holders and short-term visitors generally do not qualify. If you are entering on a visitor’s permit, your household goods are treated as standard commercial imports and taxed accordingly — which usually makes shipping a whole household financially pointless.

Timing matters a lot. Menaje de casa paperwork is tied to the date you establish residency in Colombia. Goods typically must arrive within a narrow window (commonly within a few months before or after your arrival, depending on the regime and visa type). Have your mover file the aviso de arribo and engage a customs broker before the container leaves North America — waiting until the ship docks is how people end up with storage fees and rejected exemptions.

What Counts as a Menaje de Casa

The exemption covers used household items in quantities consistent with one family’s personal use. Typical allowed items:

  • Furniture, mattresses, rugs
  • Kitchenware, small appliances, dishes, cutlery
  • Used clothing, shoes, personal linens, towels
  • Books, artwork, hobby and sports equipment
  • One used refrigerator, one used washer, one used dryer, etc. — “one of each major appliance” is the guiding principle
  • Personal electronics (laptops, tablets, cameras) in reasonable quantities
  • Tools and light workshop equipment for personal use

What’s Excluded from the Exemption

  • New items (still in original packaging or with tags) — these are treated as commercial imports
  • Duplicate appliances (two refrigerators, three TVs)
  • Quantities inconsistent with personal use (10 laptops, 50 pairs of shoes still in boxes)
  • Vehicles, motorcycles, and boats — separate regime, see section 5
  • Commercial inventory, raw materials, or anything clearly for resale
  • Firearms and ammunition (separately regulated and generally prohibited without a permit)
  • Alcohol and tobacco above personal allowance

The Packing List (Lista de Empaque)

Your itemized packing list is the core document of the menaje process. It must:

  • List every box by number
  • Describe the contents of each box in reasonable detail (not just “miscellaneous”)
  • State approximate value per box or major item in USD
  • Indicate condition (used) and approximate age where relevant
  • Be in Spanish, or bilingual — your customs broker will usually prepare the Spanish version from your English list, but start with an accurate English version
  • Match what’s actually inside the boxes — DIAN inspectors do random spot-checks and mismatches trigger full inspections

Photograph serial numbers of electronics, appliances, and power tools before packing. Colombian customs occasionally requests them to verify an item is used and not newly purchased, and it’s far easier than digging through boxes on arrival.

4. Personal Items and Small Shipments

Not every move requires a shipping container. Many people relocating to Barranquilla ship a few boxes ahead and arrive with checked luggage.

Unaccompanied Baggage

If you’re flying into Colombia and shipping a handful of boxes separately, most airlines offer “unaccompanied baggage” service — essentially air freight that rides along with commercial passenger flights. Rates are typically $5–$12 per kg with minimums of 50–100 kg. You’ll still need to clear it through customs at BAQ or BOG; the airline doesn’t do this for you.

Checked Luggage as “Personal Effects”

Each passenger can bring personal effects as checked luggage under their ticket’s baggage allowance without import duty, provided the items are clearly for personal use and not in commercial quantities. This is the cheapest way to move clothes, personal electronics, and paperwork. Most international airlines to Colombia allow 1–2 checked bags at 23 kg each in economy, with additional bags available for purchase.

Courier Shipments (DHL, FedEx, UPS)

For 1–5 boxes totaling under ~50 kg, courier is almost always the right choice. What to know:

  • De minimis threshold: Colombia allows duty-free import of personal items under a certain declared value (the threshold has historically been around USD $200, but it changes — confirm with your courier at time of shipping). Above that, standard duties and 19% IVA apply.
  • Prohibited items list: Couriers will not ship firearms, certain electronics with encryption, perishables without Invima clearance, or bulk cosmetics/supplements.
  • Declared value matters: Under-declaring to reduce duties is illegal, and couriers increasingly flag suspiciously low values. Over-declaring is also a mistake — duties are ad valorem.
  • Broker fees: Some couriers tack on a clearance fee (USD $20–$60) in addition to duties. Ask for an “all-in” landed cost before shipping.

What Not to Ship

Certain items are either prohibited, heavily restricted, or simply not worth shipping:

  • Large appliances from North America with 110V motors: Colombia uses 110V/60Hz (same as the US), so most US appliances work. However, items with dual-voltage motors or Canadian specifications may need adaptation. Always check the nameplate.
  • US-spec electronics that rely on US-only services: Smart TVs, cable boxes, and certain gaming consoles sometimes have region locks.
  • Hazardous materials: Paints, solvents, propane tanks, pool chemicals, batteries above a certain size. These are restricted in international freight generally.
  • Food and agricultural products: Seeds, soil, fresh produce, meat, and dairy are prohibited or require Invima/ICA clearance.

5. Vehicles and Motorcycles

If one section of this guide will save you money, it’s probably this one.

Colombia severely restricts used vehicle imports. As a general rule, importing a used car into Colombia for personal use by an ordinary resident is not permitted. This is not a paperwork obstacle — it’s a fundamental policy designed to protect the domestic auto industry. Most people relocating to Barranquilla sell their car in North America and buy a comparable vehicle in Colombia.

The Narrow Exceptions

Imports are possible under specific circumstances:

  • Diplomatic personnel and accredited international organizations can import vehicles under diplomatic franchise.
  • New vehicles imported by authorized dealers or by individuals through approved import channels, paying all duties and taxes (typically 35%+ landed).
  • Colombian returnees under specific programs have at times had access to limited vehicle-import benefits — these programs come and go, so verify current status with a Colombian customs broker before assuming eligibility.
  • Classic/antique vehicles (generally 35+ years old) have separate rules and may be importable as collector items.
  • Motorcycles face similar restrictions as cars; most residents buy locally.

If You Can Import: The Process

  1. Confirm eligibility in writing from a licensed Colombian customs broker before shipping anything.
  2. Obtain import license from the Ministry of Commerce where required.
  3. Ship via Ro-Ro or container. Ro-Ro is cheaper but exposes the vehicle to weather and port handling; containerized shipping ($2,800–$4,500) is safer.
  4. Pay duties and taxes. Total landed cost typically includes import duty (variable by engine size and vehicle category), 19% IVA, and a consumption tax on higher-displacement or luxury vehicles. Budget for 35–80% of vehicle value in taxes.
  5. Homologation and registration. The vehicle must be homologated (technical certification that it meets Colombian standards) and registered with the local traffic authority in Barranquilla. License plates, SOAT mandatory insurance, and annual impuesto vehicular apply.

Practical Advice

  • Sell before you go. For 95% of people relocating, this is the right answer. A comparable used vehicle in Colombia will cost you less total than importing yours.
  • Used cars hold value in Colombia. Because of import restrictions, used cars depreciate more slowly than in North America. Factor this into “buy in Colombia” budgeting — an older vehicle that would sell for $4,000 in the US might list for $8,000–$10,000 in Barranquilla.
  • Brand availability. Renault, Chevrolet, Mazda, Toyota, Kia, Hyundai, and Nissan dominate. European luxury brands and pickup trucks are available but priced at a premium.

6. Pets: Dogs, Cats, and Birds

Colombia welcomes companion animals, and bringing your pet to Barranquilla is very doable — but the paperwork is time-sensitive and a single missing endorsement can mean quarantine at your expense or denial of entry. Start six to eight weeks before departure.

Dogs and Cats: The Requirements

  1. Microchip (ISO 11784/11785 compliant, 15-digit). Strongly recommended; some airlines require it. Install before the rabies vaccination so the chip number is recorded on the rabies certificate.
  2. Rabies vaccination. Must be administered at least 30 days before travel and not more than 12 months prior (or within the vaccine’s stated validity). Puppies and kittens under 3 months are generally not eligible for travel.
  3. Core vaccinations. For dogs: distemper, parvovirus, adenovirus, parainfluenza (DHPP). For cats: feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, panleukopenia (FVRCP).
  4. Internal and external parasite treatment. Performed within a set window before travel (commonly 15–30 days) and recorded by the vet.
  5. Health certificate. Issued by a USDA-accredited veterinarian (US) or a CFIA-accredited veterinarian (Canada), dated within 10 days of travel.
  6. Endorsement by USDA APHIS / CFIA. The health certificate must be endorsed by the relevant national authority after the vet issues it. In the US, this is done through APHIS Veterinary Services (VEHCS online system). In Canada, CFIA endorses.
  7. ICA notification. Colombia’s ICA should be notified in advance of the pet’s arrival. Your broker or airline can help, or you can file directly.

Flying In With Your Pet

  • In-cabin is usually restricted to pets under 8–9 kg including carrier, and only on certain routes. Book the pet as you book your own ticket — airlines cap the number of animals per flight.
  • Checked baggage (accompanied) is available on some airlines and routes but increasingly restricted, especially for brachycephalic breeds (pugs, bulldogs, Persian cats).
  • Cargo is the most reliable option for larger animals. Your pet travels as manifest cargo, not luggage, and requires a clearance agent on the Colombian side. Expect $500–$2,000 depending on size and route.

On Arrival in Barranquilla or Bogotá

Most pets clear through Bogotá (BOG) and connect onward to Barranquilla, though direct international arrivals at BAQ do happen on certain carriers. At the airport:

  • ICA reviews the documents and physically inspects the pet.
  • Quarantine is not routine for compliant animals, but ICA may hold non-compliant pets at a certified facility at the owner’s expense until paperwork is sorted.
  • If clearing at BOG with onward travel to BAQ, factor in domestic pet transport (another $80–$200) and connection times of several hours.

Birds and Exotic Pets

Birds face significantly stricter rules due to avian influenza concerns and CITES regulations. Importation is subject to ICA permits, export permits from the origin country, and sometimes quarantine. Some parrot species are CITES-listed and effectively impossible to import as pets. If you have a bird, talk to ICA and a wildlife-trade attorney before you ship anything else.

Pet Supplies

Bring a month’s supply of prescription medication in original containers with your vet’s documentation. Prescription pet food and specialty items (hypoallergenic kibble, raw-feeding supplies) are available in Barranquilla but cost more and selection is narrower than in North American suburbs. Premium brands like Royal Canin and Hill’s are widely stocked.

7. Costs and Getting Quotes

Shipping costs change constantly with fuel prices, exchange rates, and port congestion. These ranges reflect typical early-2026 market conditions; always get three current quotes before committing.

Scenario Typical all-in cost (USD)
Studio / 1-bedroom, LCL sea freight, door-to-door $1,500–$3,500
2-bedroom apartment, 20′ FCL, door-to-door $4,500–$7,000
3–4 bedroom house, 40′ FCL, door-to-door $7,500–$14,000
5–10 boxes, air freight (500 kg) $3,000–$6,000
1–3 courier boxes (20 kg each) $250–$800
Pet, dog in cargo $500–$2,000
Duties on non-menaje goods (typical) 10–15% duty + 19% IVA

What an Honest Quote Contains

If you don’t see these line items broken out, the quote isn’t complete:

  • Origin packing and loading
  • Export customs and documentation
  • Ocean or air freight
  • Destination port handling / terminal handling charges (THC)
  • Customs brokerage in Colombia
  • Import duties and IVA (or a statement that these are not included if shipping under menaje)
  • Inland transport from port to residence
  • Delivery, unloading, and optional unpacking
  • Insurance (and the coverage limit)

Watch for quotes that exclude “destination charges” or “port fees at destination.” These can exceed $1,000 and are often added after your goods are already at sea, when you have no leverage.

8. Timelines: What to Do and When

8–12 weeks out

  • Decide scope: what ships, what you sell, what you travel with.
  • Get three written quotes from international movers.
  • Start the visa process if you haven’t already — the menaje exemption is tied to your visa status.
  • Microchip your pet (if not already) and schedule vet visits.

4–8 weeks out

  • Book the mover. Reserve a packing date.
  • Begin the pet paperwork: core vaccines, rabies booster if needed.
  • Begin inventorying. Photograph serial numbers of electronics and appliances.
  • Draft the packing list in English; leave space to add box numbers during packing.

2–4 weeks out

  • Confirm mover’s packing day and destination address in Barranquilla.
  • Schedule final vet visit for health certificate (must be within 10 days of travel).
  • Confirm APHIS/CFIA endorsement appointment or online submission.
  • Arrange temporary housing in Barranquilla if goods will arrive after you.

Week of departure

  • Packing day: be present, number every box as it’s packed, update the packing list in real time.
  • Sign the bill of lading and review the inventory the mover’s crew produces.
  • Pick up endorsed pet health certificate.
  • Confirm import broker in Colombia has the complete document set.

After arrival in Barranquilla

  • Meet your customs broker and provide any remaining documents (passport stamps, visa copy, cédula de extranjería once issued).
  • Track the vessel via the shipping line’s website.
  • Plan for a multi-day window between vessel arrival and home delivery — customs clearance typically takes 5–15 business days after unloading.

9. Documents Checklist

Core Identity & Status Documents

  • Passport (and copies of photo page and entry stamp)
  • Colombian visa (M, R, or applicable category)
  • Cédula de extranjería once issued (not required at shipment, but usually required before cargo releases)
  • Power of attorney (poder) authorizing your customs broker, notarized and apostilled

Shipment Documents

  • Ocean Bill of Lading (B/L) or Air Waybill (AWB)
  • Commercial invoice or valuation statement
  • Detailed packing list with box numbers and approximate values
  • Origin inventory (from the moving crew)
  • Insurance certificate

Menaje de Casa-Specific

  • Declaration of intent to move (declaración juramentada before a notary, sometimes required)
  • Proof of prior residence abroad (utility bills, lease, employment record — for returnees)
  • Proof of new residence in Colombia (lease or property title)

Pet Documents

  • International Health Certificate endorsed by USDA APHIS or CFIA
  • Rabies vaccination certificate
  • Core vaccination record
  • Parasite treatment record
  • Microchip certificate
  • Airline-approved carrier receipt

10. Customs Clearance at Puerto de Barranquilla

Once your container arrives at the port, the clock starts. Colombian customs allows a limited free-storage period (typically a few days) after which demurrage and storage fees accrue quickly. A good broker will have most paperwork pre-filed so clearance begins immediately on arrival.

Clearance Steps

  1. Vessel arrival and discharge. The container is unloaded at Puerto de Barranquilla.
  2. Customs declaration filed. Your broker submits the import declaration to DIAN with the full document set.
  3. Risk classification. DIAN assigns a channel:
    • Canal verde (green): documentary review only, fastest release
    • Canal amarillo (yellow): document scrutiny, possible questions
    • Canal rojo (red): physical inspection, container is opened and contents verified
  4. Inspection (if applicable). A red-channel inspection can take anywhere from a half-day to several days depending on volume. You or your broker may be required to attend.
  5. Payment. Any assessed duties, taxes, storage, or handling fees are paid through the broker.
  6. Release order. DIAN authorizes the container to leave the port.
  7. Delivery. Trucking to your residence in Barranquilla, unloading, and optional unpacking.

If DIAN Questions Your Menaje

Common triggers for questions or red-channel selection:

  • Items in new condition (retail packaging, plastic wrap, tags)
  • Duplicate appliances or quantities inconsistent with personal use
  • Missing or vague packing list
  • High-value items without documentation of prior ownership
  • Discrepancy between declared and observed contents

If a portion of your shipment is disallowed under menaje, you generally have options: pay the standard import duties and IVA on those items, abandon them at the port, or re-export them (which almost never makes economic sense). Your broker will walk you through the math.

11. Prohibited and Restricted Items

Absolutely Prohibited

  • Narcotics and precursor chemicals
  • Counterfeit goods and pirated media
  • Endangered species products (ivory, certain leathers, coral, CITES-listed plants/animals)
  • Obscene or offensive material as defined under Colombian law
  • Used clothing in commercial quantity (personal quantities under menaje are fine)

Restricted (Requires Permit or Special Handling)

  • Firearms and ammunition: Strictly controlled; permits are difficult to obtain and generally limited to specific categories of residents. Do not ship.
  • Medications and supplements: Prescription quantities for personal use are usually fine with a doctor’s letter. Bulk quantities require Invima registration.
  • Food products: Most packaged food for personal use in small quantities is tolerated, but ICA and Invima regulate imports. Avoid meat, dairy, fresh produce, and seeds.
  • Plants and plant products: ICA phytosanitary certificate required.
  • Cultural and artistic heritage items: Antiques, religious artifacts, and artworks may require declarations.
  • Drones: Can be imported but must be registered with Aerocivil before use.
  • Ham/amateur radio equipment: Requires a local operator’s license.

Practical Items to Leave Behind

Beyond legal restrictions, some items just aren’t worth shipping:

  • Mattresses — heavy, bulky, replaceable cheaply in Colombia
  • Inexpensive Ikea-style furniture — often worth less than the shipping cost
  • Half-empty toiletries, cleaning supplies, pantry staples — prohibited or not worth it
  • Large backyard equipment (grills, lawnmowers) — Caribbean climate and local construction mean different needs

12. Choosing a Mover or Freight Forwarder

The mover you choose matters more than the shipping method. A cheap mover with a weak Colombian partner can cost you thousands in port fees, missed menaje deadlines, and damage claims. A good mover with a Barranquilla agent pays for itself.

Questions to Ask Every Quote

  1. Who is your destination agent in Barranquilla? (Ask for name and contact — call them.)
  2. Is the quote port-to-port or door-to-door, and are destination port charges included?
  3. Is customs brokerage included, or do I engage my own?
  4. How do you handle the menaje de casa exemption — what do you need from me?
  5. What insurance is included, and what’s the coverage limit per item?
  6. What happens if the ship is delayed or my visa isn’t issued on time?
  7. How long does clearance typically take at Barranquilla, and what are typical storage fees if it overruns?
  8. Can you provide three recent customer references for Colombia shipments?

Red Flags

  • No written itemized quote — just a total
  • Vague about destination agent or customs broker
  • Unwilling to provide customer references
  • Pushes you to sign quickly “before rates change”
  • Cash-only or untraceable payment method
  • Not registered with FIDI, FAIM, IAM, or a comparable industry accreditation

Accreditations Worth Looking For

Industry certifications aren’t a guarantee, but they filter out the worst operators:

  • FIDI / FAIM — the international standard for household goods movers
  • IAM (International Association of Movers) membership
  • AMSA / ProMover for US-based firms
  • CAM (Canadian Association of Movers) for Canada-based firms
  • Local accreditation of the Colombian partner with ANDI or similar

Colombian-Side Customs Brokers (Agentes de Aduanas)

Your North American mover usually has a preferred destination agent in Colombia. If you want an independent broker — or if you’re handling the import yourself — start with brokers registered with DIAN and active at Puerto de Barranquilla. Several of the Colombian logistics firms listed above (Aviomar, Expotrans, Almaviva, SIT) operate their own in-house customs-brokerage arms. Independent specialists include ABC Cargo Logistics and Pan Global Logistics Colombia, plus numerous smaller firms with offices at Puerto de Barranquilla. Vet any broker by asking for:

  • Their DIAN license number (código de agente de aduanas)
  • Three recent references from household-goods clients
  • Written all-in quote for port handling, brokerage, duties (if applicable), and inland delivery
  • Clarity on what happens if your menaje is partially rejected

On scam movers: Unfortunately, international moving attracts fraudsters. Warning signs: extremely low quotes that undercut everyone else by 30%+, no physical business address, demanding large deposits via wire transfer or cryptocurrency, no FMC license (for US-based ocean movers), no written contract. The US Federal Maritime Commission (fmc.gov) lets you verify a mover’s license before paying anything.

13. Insurance

Household-goods shipping insurance is not optional — at least not for anyone who’d feel the loss. Sea freight involves ports, cranes, trucks, humidity, and occasionally theft; air freight is gentler but still has handling damage. Couriers include basic liability, but it’s usually a flat per-kg figure that doesn’t cover real value.

Two Coverage Models

  • Total-value coverage: You declare the total value of the shipment and pay a premium (typically 2–4% of insured value). Claims pay out up to the declared value, minus deductible.
  • Itemized high-value coverage: High-value items (art, musical instruments, heirlooms) are listed and valued individually, often with appraisal documentation. Standard household items are covered under the blanket policy.

What Usually Isn’t Covered

  • Items you packed yourself (“PBO” — packed by owner). If you want full coverage, the mover must pack it.
  • Electronics internal damage (screens work on arrival, but images are degraded — this is famously hard to claim)
  • Items not listed on the inventory
  • Mold and moisture damage (depends on policy and whether container was breached)
  • War, strike, and confiscation by government authorities — sometimes available as a rider

Document Before You Ship

Before the moving crew arrives, photograph and video every room, every high-value item, and every known pre-existing damage. Keep receipts and appraisals for items valued over $1,000. A five-minute phone walkthrough before packing day has saved countless insurance claims.

14. Common Pitfalls (Real Mistakes, Real Money)

  1. Shipping before the visa is issued. Your goods arrive, you don’t qualify for menaje, and the container sits at the port racking up storage while you scramble. Ship after you have the visa or a firm timeline, not before.
  2. Over-declaring “new” items. Anything in retail packaging with tags is taxable. Remove tags and packaging for items that are genuinely personal use — don’t lie, but don’t gift-wrap DIAN’s case against you either.
  3. Under-insuring. Declaring a $40,000 household at $10,000 to save on premiums means a partial-loss claim covers a quarter of what you lost.
  4. Ignoring the destination agent. The US or Canadian mover isn’t the one clearing your goods; the Barranquilla agent is. Call them. Their English isn’t the point — their responsiveness is.
  5. Packing hazardous materials. Aerosols, propane, opened paint, lithium-ion batteries outside devices. These cause container holds, fines, and sometimes shipment seizure.
  6. Under-estimating the Colombian side. Customs takes longer than you expect. Plan 4–8 weeks between arrival and having everything in your apartment.
  7. Bringing furniture that won’t fit. Barranquilla apartments often have narrow stairwells and small elevators. Measure your doorway and elevator dimensions before shipping an oversized sectional.
  8. Forgetting the climate. Barranquilla is hot and humid year-round. Leather, paper, and certain woods suffer. If you’re shipping a Steinway or a library, plan for climate-controlled storage.
  9. Not budgeting for last-mile delivery. Port release is not the same as “in your living room.” Inland trucking, stair-carry fees, and unpacking are often billed separately.
  10. Assuming the pet paperwork is handled. Airline staff will check that documents exist; ICA checks that they are correct. Read every requirement yourself.

15. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the whole process take, door to door?

From packing day in North America to unpacked in Barranquilla, budget 8–14 weeks for sea freight and 3–5 weeks for air freight. This includes packing, transit, customs, and final delivery. Courier shipments are 1–2 weeks.

Should I ship my car?

For 95% of people, no. Colombia restricts used-vehicle imports and the math rarely works out. Sell your vehicle in North America and buy in Colombia after you arrive. The exception is diplomatic staff and a narrow band of returning Colombians under specific programs.

Will my US appliances work in Colombia?

Colombia uses 110V/60Hz, the same as the United States, so most US appliances work without converters. Canadian appliances are typically the same. Check the nameplate for dual-voltage capability. Items with 220V-only specs (common in some dryers, ranges) may not be usable. Outlet shapes are compatible with US two-prong and three-prong plugs.

Can I ship before my visa is approved?

Technically yes, but you’d be shipping at full commercial import duty — the menaje exemption requires a qualifying visa at the time of clearance. The prudent path is to have the visa in hand or stamped before goods leave the US or Canadian port.

What’s the cheapest way to ship a few boxes?

Unaccompanied baggage through your airline is often the cheapest for 50–150 kg. Couriers like DHL are most convenient for 1–3 boxes. USPS or Canada Post can work for documents and very small items but are unreliable for anything valuable.

Do I need a Spanish-speaking broker?

Your Colombian customs broker will operate entirely in Spanish. If you don’t speak Spanish, your North American mover or destination agent will relay, but cutting out the translation layer (either by learning enough Spanish for basic back-and-forth or by hiring a bilingual broker) saves time and prevents misunderstandings.

What happens if my container is red-channeled?

A customs officer opens the container and inspects the contents against your packing list. If everything matches, clearance proceeds with a modest delay (1–5 business days typically) and possibly an inspection fee. If there are discrepancies, expect negotiation, potential duties on affected items, or in rare cases, fines.

Can I pack the boxes myself?

Yes, but it usually voids most of your insurance coverage on the packed contents. For valuable or fragile items, let the movers pack. A mixed approach — you pack books and clothing, the movers pack kitchen, electronics, and artwork — is common and balances cost with coverage.

How much cash should I budget for surprise fees?

Add 10–15% to your total quoted cost as a reserve. Typical surprises: longer-than-expected customs clearance (storage fees), red-channel inspection fees, last-mile access issues (stair-carry, long-carry, shuttle fees if the truck can’t reach your door), and currency fluctuations between quote and payment.

Is it safe to ship valuables?

Generally yes, with insurance and a reputable mover. For irreplaceable items (wedding rings, passports, critical medications, small heirlooms) carry them on your person. For medium-value items (laptops, jewelry), air freight or courier with full declared value is safer than ocean freight.

What about my plants?

Plants require ICA phytosanitary certificates and inspection, and most houseplants aren’t worth the trouble. Seeds are prohibited or strictly regulated. Give plants to friends, buy new ones in Barranquilla — the selection is excellent and the prices are low.

What about alcohol or a wine collection?

Small personal quantities (a bottle or two per adult) are generally fine under personal allowance. A wine collection is treated as a commercial import with duties and IVA, plus potentially a specific-consumption tax. Most collectors find it’s cheaper to sell at origin and rebuild in Colombia.

16. A Short Checklist You Can Screenshot

  1. Confirm visa status and timeline
  2. Get three written, itemized quotes
  3. Verify destination agent in Barranquilla
  4. Microchip pet; schedule rabies booster if needed
  5. Inventory and photograph everything
  6. Prepare packing list (English and Spanish)
  7. Buy adequate insurance
  8. Schedule APHIS/CFIA pet endorsement
  9. Pack day: number boxes, update list in real time
  10. Fly with documents, not in the container
  11. Meet customs broker on arrival
  12. Be available for red-channel inspection if called
  13. Check every item against inventory on delivery
  14. File insurance claims within the policy window (usually 14–30 days)
